The idea

We can't always find the words for "not right now."

Replying takes energy. Ignoring a message feels like guilt. So we leave people on read, and carry a low hum of social pressure all evening.

Bloom turns that invisible feeling into something gentle and physical. A flower opens when you're ready for people, and folds when you need rest - and the people who care about you simply see it, the way you'd notice a friend's mood across a room.

How it works

Three quiet gestures

01

Lock your phone into the flower

Coming home should mean coming home. Rest your phone in the flower's stand and it stays there - a small physical boundary between you and the endless scroll. Screen time drops because the screen is, simply, out of reach.

  • physical boundary
  • less screen time
  • a ritual, not an app
02

Fold your petals to set your availability

Open the petals when you're up for people; fold them when you're running low. Folding the flower sets a timer for how long you'll be unreachable - it mutes your notifications for that window, while letting a whitelist of emergency contacts always reach you, so disconnecting never feels unsafe.

  • timed quiet
  • notifications muted
  • emergency whitelist
03

Check on your friends' meadow

Your friends are flowers too. Together you make a meadow - a quiet glance shows who's open for a chat, who's winding down, and who's resting. You learn who might take a while to reply before you reach out, so no one feels ignored and no one feels pressured.

  • social circle
  • no read receipts
  • empathy at a glance

The process · human-centered design

Made with people, not just for them

Bloom grew through short, iterative loops - each one grounded in two things: speculative design critiques that questioned how digital devices should live in people's emotional life, and a concrete user study on a working prototype.

Speculative critique → Recyclable prototypes → User study ↺
01 — Speculate & critique

Question the idea before building it

We started in paper. Folded blooms, foam centers and brainstorming cards let us stage provocative "what if" versions and critique them with peers - does a flower that speaks for you feel caring, or controlling? These speculative critiques shaped what our device should and shouldn't do before any electronics.

  • paper & foam mock-ups
  • "what if" provocations
  • peer critique
02 — Prototype in recyclable materials

Make the petals actually move

Each feedback session fed a new design. We prototyped with low-waste, recyclable materials, and Arduino - an open-source electronic platform that enables the flower to truly fold and unfold.

  • recyclable PLA bioplastic
  • cardboard mechanism
  • ARDUINO
03 — USER STUDY

A user study on the prototype

Validating a concept this with real users is what turns a thoughtful idea into a trustworthy product - so we put our flower in front of the people it's designed for.

We planned a small pilot study with five to eight young adults who can be considered digital natives. Each person tries the flower, thinks aloud, then answers a short questionnaire and a quick interview - using the prototype, concept images and everyday scenarios.

How do people perceive a physical flower that shows social availability — and could it ease setting our digital boundaries?

— the question this pilot set out to answer
  • Hypothesis 01

    The flower is intuitive and easy to understand.

    People grasp what the petal states mean and how it shows availability - without instructions.

  • Hypothesis 02

    It's an emotionally supportive, socially acceptable way to share availability.

    Showing your state through the flower feels calming and comfortable in everyday life.

  • Hypothesis 03

    It encourages conscious disconnection from devices.

    The physical object nudges people toward less phone-checking and less digital overload.
